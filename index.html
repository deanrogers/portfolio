MAJOR GOAL: PLANNING, ESTABLISHMENT AND DEVELOPMENT OF PB4L-SW

Reflection...

The opportunity to drive foundational positive cultural change in a school occurs rarely. It has been an ongoing privilege, and a challenge, to be intimately involved in the establishment and evolution of PB4L and deeper student welfare systems at Ross Intermediate School as leader of the “programme” and coach for staff from the outset (including a year’s full release and a .4 secondment to the Ministry for 12 months).

The embedding of PB4L is a five year process (we are in year four) and it has been critical to keep “the long game” in view, seeding ideas and systems and allowing others to nurture and implement them, encouraging an inclusiveness of consultation, success and professional recognition. Through the process I have worked with reluctant adopters, “unconscious incompetents” and idealist dreamers - all bringing their own flavour to the consultations and resulting actions.
Establishing an evidence-based behaviour management “system” (it is so much more than that) that is behaviourist-centred, and encourages restorative practices and active de- escalation techniques, has foundationally altered our school. The level of professional discussion and support, the positive interactions (staff-staff, staff-student, student-student) and a sense of calm, even during acute incidents, are entirely tangible outcomes commented on by staff, whanāu and visitors. The “PB4L Way” has influenced our annual goals and plans, reporting processes and now our appraisal procedures, with interactions and plans based around our three core statements of Whakamahia kia Tika (Act with Integrity), Ako kia Kaha (Learn with Purpose) and Whaia ou Moemoeā (Inquire and Dream).
Throughout the last 3 - 4 years  I have learnt valuable lessons in diplomacy, dealing with school governance and leadership, allowing failure to fuel innovation (the redevelopment process around our “Matrix” being a prime example) and the need to recognise and celebrate the progress of individuals and the larger corporate group.
But more fundamentally, the introduction and evolution of PB4L has resulted in a greater ability of staff and students to focus on our core aim - improving student learning outcomes.

Coaches Corner - WHAT MAKES A GOOD TEACHER?
(by Dean, with thanks to Billie Chote, Juliet Lewis and other wise heads)

I am convinced that there are three things absolutely essential to effective learning and teaching and the implied
role of both the student and teacher.
They are:
Relationships, Relationships, and Relationships.
Nothing is more critical to success in the education process, whether that be academic or behavioural, than the
relationship between learner and teacher - a truly effective relationship will transcend the lack of resources,
learning space evolution (MLE’s), inconsistency of outside support and all the other distractions we are challenged
with daily. So, how do we build the strong, reciprocal relationships required?

A classroom is a place of words and actions. As teachers, I think we need to constantly strive to develop positive,
supportive and challenging words and actions that motivate kids to “go beyond themselves” - to Learn with
Purpose and Inquire and Dream... We need to consistently assess our own words and actions to ensure we are
treating students with dignity and respect, while encouraging them to do the same, and so we work together to
meet our respective academic and behavioural goals - āheinga!

Ok, that all sounds nice but what does it actually look like in a classroom? A slight diversion here - “classroom” is
not restricted to the four walled box you unlock each morning...
Below are some ideas for encouraging mutually respectful and supportive relationships with your kids...

When verbally encouraging students:

• Be clear and specific by only describing the skill you have observed. Don’t refer to past mistakes the student
has made or wishes for their future behaviour.

• Keep your words appropriate to the individual situation. For example, you should know your students well
enough to be able to differentiate whether public or private recognition is more reinforcing for them.







• Be genuine by finding your own style to communicate sincere encouragement, especially when working with
mature students.

• Give encouragement only immediately after the desired behaviour, rather than using it as a general motivator.

• Give it frequently during acquisition of the desired behaviour, that is, at a high enough rate to change or
maintain behaviours.

When dealing with specific students who have made a behavioural error, to expose and get behind thinking, try:

“Help me understand what you were thinking when you said/did...”
“What did you mean by...?”
“How did you know?”
“How did you figure that out?”

To confirm your own understanding of the speaker’s statement:

“Let’s see if I’ve got this right” (Then briefly summarise your understanding of the speaker’s statement).
“So you think that...?”
“It sounds to me like you’re saying.... am I understanding your comments correctly?”

To elicit extension or expression of thinking:

“How else might we think about...?”
“Can you give me an example?”
“What if...?”
“I’d like to hear more of your thinking”

To encourage self-assessment:

“What did you learn when...?”
“What can we learn from this?”
“How does this work for you?

So, we need to constantly reflect on our words and actions (and the sincerity and tone of both) so that we can
encourage strong learning relationships with our kids and colleagues.

COLLABORATIVE LEADERSHIP (Minor Goal)

Personal Reflection

While my style of leadership has always been collaborative rather than authoritative or hierarchal, over the last two to three years our entire school has begun to reflect that approach with a planned, legislated move to “Professional Collaborative Pairs” and coaching models in management.


Prior to this school-wide management initiative, I quietly modelled and seeded the ideas in both the management actions in my teaching team and in the informal and formal professional discussions with senior staff and influencers. The initiative started to take root when I was fully released in 2015 to focus on student welfare. My “long game”, supportive approach to managing student behaviour and attitudes allowed me to work closely with staff, sometimes in acute crisis situations, and I was able to model a staff<->student and staff<->staff leadership approach which was generally unexpected and well received by most.


My personal mantra (yes, it gets repeated frequently at school!) is, “...every experience is a learning opportunity...” and this feeds my interactions with and management of staff and students. The coaching aspect cannot be understated and certainly within my teaching team there has been an evolving culture of professional and personal support, loyalty and willingness to step outside comfort zones. This is reflected in teachers asking to remain in my team from year to year (in spite of some difficult conversations), something not previously common at my school.


This evolution of staff and student interactional culture into authentic collaboration has grown in parallel with the introduction of PB4L-SW, but is something even more foundational. It has positively affected learning interactions and professional relationships. However, it is not an accomplishment that was legislated at the outset, in fact the pervading culture was far from the eventual emerging outcome. I have worked quietly to improve what I see as an essential area of our institutional character and it has been extremely satisfying to see it become adopted and owned by leadership. True collaboration is still a way away, but we are definitely moving in the right direction and I’m quietly proud of my contribution to that.
